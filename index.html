The decline in the economic assimilation of immigrants¶

Introduction¶

Borjas 2015 documents the decline in the rate of wage growth and rate of English acquisition of the more recent immigrant cohorts (compared to those migrated during the early 1960s - defined as the early cohort). He postulates that there is a connection between these two phenomena, pointing toward the growing pre-existing immigrant population as a potential source of the decline. To quote his words,

In fact, the study identifies one factor that seems to explain part of the decline in both the rate of economic assimilation and the rate of English language acquisition: the rapid growth in the size of specific national origin groups in the United States. New immigrants are less likely to assimilate, and to invest in human capital the larger the pre-existing size of the national origin group in the United States.

Cassidy 2019 provides additional evidence of the phenomenon, "the occupational assimilation toward natives in task requirements observed for earlier cohorts has slowed significantly for more recent cohorts, making a similar conjecture as Borjas, that is the increase in size of country of origin and linguistic groups explains the decline.

I, however, find this conjecture is not sensible. As Borjas pointed out in his paper, the initial wage gap with natives at arrival of the more recent cohorts does not significantly differ from that of earlier cohorts (See Table 2 of Borjas 2015), the slower wage growth rate means the welfare of the more recent cohorts are also lower than that of those arrived earlier. If the more recent cohorts can simply choose away from the pre-existing immigrants so that they would be in a similar local economy to the earlier cohort and experience more wage growth, why wouldn't they? And perhaps the only evidence that would support Borjas' and Cassidy's conjecture is when the initial wage gap of the more recent immigrant cohorts is smaller than those came earlier and hence, as the marginal cost of time to learn English of the more recent cohorts is higher, they put less effort into learning English, enjoying a smoother consumption profile.

In my "Diversity, Growth, and Inequality" paper, I analyze the effect of social/communication friction that arises in a diverse economy on human capital accumulation. An implication of the paper is that the stronger friction would lower the human capital acquisition rate of the minority group. In the immigrant scenario, that means higher social friction would lower the human capital assimilation rate of immigrants. Furthermore, higher friction also leads to wider human capital gap between immigrants and the natives, on the balanced growth path (See Theorem 1 and Proposition 2). In my paper Immigrants, Language Barrier, and Assimilation, I find that both the implied communication friction is stronger for the more recent cohorts and the decline in their human capital accumulation, most significant for immigrants with lower educations (See Table 15 and Table 17)

From my work, a sensible explanation for the observed decline in the economic assimilation of the more recent newcomers is the increase in social friction, between immigrants and natives.

Meanwhile, Fouka, Mazumder, and Tabellini 2019 document the change in the attitude of white natives toward black and Hispanic due to the change in the immigrant population over time. A quote from their paper,

Table 2 provides evidence that Mexican immigrants are a group more distant from whites than are native-born blacks, and that the increase in their numbers further increases this relative effective distance. A theory of social categorization would then predict that Mexican immigration increases the salience of immigrant status (and lowers the salience of skin color) as a relevant dimension of social divisions.

Three distinct perspectives point toward the following hypothesis: The decline of the economic assimilation of the more recent immigrant cohorts is due to three potential factors (i) higher social friction between natives and more recent immigrants due to the change in their attitude toward the newcomers (ii) higher entry cost to the skilled segment of labor market for the more recent newcomers and (iii) the lack of support for the post-migration human capital development of the more recent newcomers (more recent newcomers are more likely to locate in non-traditional immigrant states and in 2016, more than 32 states reported that they were in shortage of teachers for English Language Leaners or ELL students).

In this research proposal, I provide some evidence that social friction is higher for the more recent immigrant cohorts compared to those who migrated during the early 60s.

Attitude of native toward immigrants, a measure of social friction¶

Inspired by Baker, Bloom, and Davis (2016), I construct the metrics of native's attitude toward immigrants using historical newspaper data from the Library of Congress (part I and II) and historical articles from The Economist. The two metrics include:

  1. One using the normalized count of articles discussed the immigration topic. Articles that referred immigrants as newcomers are counted as favorable attitude toward newcomers while those included one of the following negative words are counted as anti-immigration: {anxiety; panic; fear; crime; terror; worry; concern; violent; illegal; undesired; excluded; fraud; outrage; prevent; prohibit; epidemic; foreigner}. The set of negative words are mostly picked from the WordCloud of words using in these historical articles.
  2. One using the sentiment score of adjustive/verb/noun words in the neighborhood of 10 words surrounding a word in the set {immigrant; migration; and similar}. The sentiment score comes from SentiWords which constructs these scores using the lexical relationship between words.

I. Count of newspaper articles¶

In order to count newspaper articles contained certain keywords, I use the provided search API with results in json format. The first element of each search query gives us the number of articles with such keywords. Normalizing the count of articles of negative attitude by dividing it by the total count of articles discussing the immigrant topic, I obtain the Anti-immigrant Index. Similarly, for the Newcomer Index.

Overall the series show a decline in the negative attitude of native toward immigrants with the Anti-immigrant Index and the opposite for the Newcomer Index. After 1960, there was a sharp decrease in the Newcomer Index and a sharp jump in the Anti-immigrant Index.

II. Sentiment toward immigrants¶

Count of newspaper articles is one way to measure native's attitude toward immigrants. It, however, words that are carefully selected that are context and time relevant. What if there was a shift toward the usage of the word friend instead of newcomer in articles favored immigration? A different and more generalized measure is the sentiment of words used in these articles. For each article, I search for an adjective within a neighborhood of 10 words from the word of interest, e.g. immigrant, migration, and use the sentiment score provided by SentiWords to rate the sentiment of each article. The average sentiment of all articles within a year is considered the a measure of native's attitude toward immigrants in that period. The variance of the sentiment score is considered as the polarization of native's attitude toward immigrants.

Shown below are the Sentiment Indices of native's attitude toward immigrants measured using the first 500 articles returned from the search query (this limit is set due to the limitation of my computational power at the moment). The two indices are computed using sentiment scores provided by SentiWords which built on top of SentiWordNet (provided for reference).

These indices provide a slightly different picture than before. The native's attitude toward immigrants seems to have declined after 1920 (after WW1) coincidence with the time Congress passed the National Origin Formula that limit immigration by country of origin. The increment of public sentiment toward immigrants after 1940 is also consistent with the earlier metrics. It is also worth noting of the sharp decline in the Polarization Indices at the end of WW1 and generally decline with respect to time.

III. Sentiment toward immigrants, data from articles in The Economist¶

Similarly, I construct the Sentiment series using the historical articles from The Economist using the first ten issues of every year (computational limitation). I also compute the trend component using the HP Filter with Lambda=1600. This is, at best, described as the Western's attitude toward immigrants. It, however, does show a decline in positive attitude toward immigrants after 1960 while the prior movement of the trend component matches that with the previous series constructed using the US newspaper data.

Forward¶

This project seems to be a natural extension of my previous works as it offers evidence to connect the theoretical predictions with data. These metrics still need more works before they can be used as controls in the regression similar to Borjas 2015 to show how much the change in native's attitude toward immigrants explain the decline in wage growth of the more recent immigrants.
